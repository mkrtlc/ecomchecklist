Open the GA4 property of any mid-size e-commerce team and you will usually find the same thing: a Looker Studio dashboard with a conversion rate tile, a revenue tile, and GA4 funnel reports that have not been touched since whoever built them left the company. Someone glances at it in the Monday meeting, nods, and moves on. Nothing about the roadmap changes.

That is not a measurement problem. It is a reporting problem. GA4 has three exploration types built specifically to answer "where is the leak and why," and almost nobody on the CRO side uses more than one of them, if any. Funnel exploration, path exploration, and segment overlap exist to turn a static conversion number into a specific, testable hypothesis.

This post is about using GA4's Explore reports the way they were designed: as a diagnostic instrument you run every week, not a chart you build once for a stakeholder deck. If your GA4 instrumentation itself is shaky, start with [our GA4 measurement playbook](/blog/ga4-measurement-playbook-consent-mode-server-side) first. This post assumes clean events and picks up at the reporting layer.

## A funnel report is a diagnostic instrument, not a scoreboard

The mental model that fixes most bad GA4 reporting: a funnel report's job is to produce a hypothesis, not a headline number.

"Our conversion rate is 2.6%" tells you nothing you can act on. "38% of mobile users who add to cart never reach begin_checkout, and the drop concentrates on the cart drawer, not the PDP" tells you exactly what to test next. The difference between those two statements is the difference between a scoreboard and a diagnostic instrument.

For context on what "normal" looks like: [Baymard Institute's aggregate research](https://baymard.com/lists/cart-abandonment-rate) across dozens of studies puts overall cart and checkout abandonment in the high-60s to low-70s percent range, with mobile consistently running higher than desktop. If your own funnel exploration shows a step-to-step drop well outside that band, in either direction, that gap is your starting hypothesis, not a number to shrug at in a Monday meeting.

A scoreboard answers "how are we doing." A diagnostic instrument answers "what, specifically, is broken, for whom, and where in the sequence." GA4's standard Reports section is built for the first question. The Explore section, specifically Funnel exploration, Path exploration, and Segment overlap, is built for the second. If your team only opens standard Reports, you are running a scoreboard operation and calling it CRO.

## The three GA4 Explore reports that matter for CRO

Before building anything, it helps to know which report answers which question. Most teams default to funnel exploration alone and stop there, which only tells you where the leak is, not why it exists or who it affects.

| Report | Question it answers | When to use it |
|---|---|---|
| Funnel exploration | Where, in a defined sequence of steps, do users drop off? | Weekly, to locate leaks and track step-to-step conversion trend |
| Path exploration | What did users actually do before or after a given step? | After a leak is located, to see the real behavior around it |
| Segment overlap | Which user groups are actually affected, and do my segment definitions overlap or double-count? | Before you scope a fix, to confirm who the problem belongs to |

Each report on its own gives you a partial picture. Funnel exploration without path exploration tells you a rate dropped but not what users did instead. Path exploration without segment overlap tells you a behavior pattern exists but not whether it is a general problem or one isolated to a specific device, channel, or product category. Run all three together and you get a hypothesis you can actually write a test brief against.

## Build the funnel exploration that finds the leak

In GA4, go to Explore, then start a new exploration from the [Funnel exploration template](https://support.google.com/analytics/answer/9327974). Define your steps as events: `view_item`, `add_to_cart`, `begin_checkout`, `add_payment_info`, `purchase`. Five steps is enough for most stores; more than seven gets hard to read. On Shopify, map these to your theme's Web Pixels events rather than guessing at parity, since quick-add and cart-drawer flows often fire a different event sequence than a standard PDP add-to-cart.

Two settings decide whether the report tells the truth or lies to you.

**Closed vs. open funnel.** A closed funnel requires users to enter at step 1 and complete each step in strict order; anyone who skips a step drops out of the funnel entirely, which understates your actual funnel population. An open funnel lets users enter at any step, which better reflects real behavior since plenty of visitors land directly on a product page from a paid ad or an email link and never see the homepage. Default to open funnels for diagnosis. Use closed funnels only when you specifically need to measure a fully linear, first-touch journey.

**Elapsed time.** Toggle "Show elapsed time" and GA4 calculates the average time between each consecutive step. A long gap between `add_to_cart` and `begin_checkout` usually means users are comparison shopping or waiting for a discount code, a very different problem than a short gap with a high drop rate, which usually means friction in the cart itself.

Once the funnel is built, click any step's bar and use the **Next Action** breakdown. Pick a dimension, typically event name or page path, and GA4 surfaces the top 5 things users did instead of continuing. A cluster of "(no next action)" rows at a given step is your clearest signal of exit, not deflection to something else.

### The weekly funnel checklist

Run this every Monday, not just when revenue looks off:

1. Open the standing funnel exploration, segmented by device (mobile vs. desktop).
2. Compare this week's step-to-step rates against a 4-week rolling average, not just last week.
3. Flag any step where the drop moved more than 3-5 percentage points.
4. Pull the Next Action breakdown on the flagged step.
5. Note whether the shift correlates with a recent deploy, app install, or theme change.
6. If the shift is real and unexplained, move to path exploration on that step.

## The Locate, Path, Overlap (LPO) diagnostic

Funnel exploration locates a leak. Path exploration explains it. Segment overlap tells you who it actually belongs to. Running all three in sequence, rather than stopping at the first, is the diagnostic loop we call **LPO**: Locate, Path, Overlap.

**Locate.** Use the weekly funnel checklist above to find the step with the largest unexplained drop. This is the "where."

**Path.** Take that exact step and open a path exploration anchored there. This is the "why," covered in detail below.

**Overlap.** Check whether the affected users form a distinct segment (mobile Safari, a specific traffic source, a product category) or whether the drop is genuinely funnel-wide. This is the "who," and it determines the scope of your fix.

Skipping Path means you locate a leak but guess at the cause. Skipping Overlap means you build a fix for "everyone" when the real problem sits in one segment, which dilutes your test and buries a real effect in noise. Once LPO gives you a located, explained, scoped hypothesis, hand it to your test prioritization process. ## Read path exploration in both directions

Path exploration has two directions and most teams only ever use one.

**Forward pathing** starts from a node you pick, say the cart drawer, and shows what users did next. This is the standard drop-off diagnosis: you locate the leak in your funnel exploration, then anchor a forward path there to see the actual next actions instead of guessing from the Next Action card alone.

**Backward (reverse) pathing** starts from an end point, usually `purchase`, and shows what led there. This is the direction most CRO teams skip, and it is often more useful. Instead of asking "why do people fail," you ask "what do people who succeed actually do." If your backward path from `purchase` consistently shows a detour through your FAQ or shipping policy page before checkout, that page is doing conversion work your funnel report would never surface on its own.

The highest-signal move here is comparing paths for two segments side by side: converting users vs. non-converting users who reached the same starting node. Apply one segment as a comparison inside the same exploration and look at where the two paths diverge. That divergence point, not the aggregate drop-off percentage, is usually the actual thing worth testing.

## Segment overlap: catching leaks that hide inside your own definitions

Segment overlap compares two or three segments as a Venn diagram, and its most underused feature is the **Breakdown** setting, which slices the overlap by a dimension like device category or traffic source.

Two practical uses for a CRO team:

**Auditing your own segment definitions.** If your "cart abandoners" segment and your "checkout abandoners" segment overlap far more than you expect, you may be double-counting the same users under two different problem statements, which inflates the apparent size of two different leaks that are actually one leak.

**Scoping the LPO fix.** Once path exploration shows you a divergence point, build a segment for the users who hit it and overlap it against device category, new vs. returning, and top traffic source. If the overlap concentrates almost entirely in one segment, for example mobile Safari sessions from paid social, you have a scoped, high-confidence hypothesis. If it is spread evenly, the problem is structural to the page, not the audience.

## Common mistakes that make funnel data lie to you

Even a well-built exploration produces bad conclusions if you misread these:

- **Sampling.** Explorations on high-traffic properties can hit query sampling thresholds around 10 million events; results become directional estimates, not exact counts. Cross-check big swings against standard Reports, which are unsampled, or move recurring high-volume queries into BigQuery once your event volume makes Explore sampling a regular problem.
- **Thresholding.** GA4 silently withholds rows with very small user counts. A real but small leak can look like "no data" rather than a suppressed row, which is easy to misread as "nothing happening here."
- **Closed funnel by default.** Teams build the funnel once with closed logic, never revisit the setting, and wonder why their numbers look worse than backend order data implies.
- **Comparing standard Reports to Explorations directly.** They can differ due to sampling, thresholding, processing lag, and consent modeling. Compare trend direction, not exact digits, across the two.
- **Ignoring overlap entirely.** A team that never runs segment overlap will eventually chase a "leak" that is really two overlapping segment definitions inflating each other.

## What this replaces on your Monday agenda

A GA4 reporting habit built around LPO does not need more dashboards. It needs three explorations, reviewed in the same order, every week: the standing funnel exploration segmented by device, a backward path from `purchase` refreshed to catch shifting behavior, and a segment overlap check on your two most commonly cited problem segments so tracking or definition drift gets caught before it corrupts a quarter's worth of trend lines.

| Cadence | Report | Output |
|---|---|---|
| Weekly | Funnel exploration (open, by device) | Located leak, flagged if drop exceeds 3-5pp |
| Weekly | Backward path from purchase | Confirmed or new divergence point |
| Bi-weekly | Segment overlap on top 2 problem segments | Scoped fix, or flag for tracking drift |
